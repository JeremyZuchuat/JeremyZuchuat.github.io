I am a PhD Economist specialised in the use of applied statistical and econometric techniques. I am interested in any topics related to labor, education, public or health economics, which can be analyzed through the lense of data analytics. In most of my work/projects, I use granular adminstrative or survey data as well as advanced statistical and econometric techniques to answer socio-economic questions.

Academic work

What Drives the Decline in Job Offers?

joint with Rafael Lalive (UNIL), Aderonke Osikominu (Hohenheim), Lorenzo Pesaresi (UZH) & Josef Zweimüller (UZH)

Studying the duration dependence phenomenon in the labor market, i.e. the effect of elapsed unemployment duration on job finding.
Analysing duration's effect on job seekers' (e.g. workers' discouragement) and firms' (e.g. discrimination towards long-term unemployed) behaviors, accounting for dynamic selection.

Commuting Decisions in Fiscally Decentralized Economies

joint with Nicola Mauri (University of Lausanne, Swiss Federal Tax Administration)

Studying the trade-offs faced by workers in fiscally decentralized areas bewteen local (income) taxes, commuting costs and housing prices.
Analysing the effects of these location factors on income segregation.

How Vacancy Referrals affect Job Search: Evidence from Job Applications Data

single-authored paper

Evaluating the effects of vacancy referrals (by caseworkers at Publics Employment Centers) on job search outcomes.
Use of granular applications data to analyze the effects of the policy on job seekers search effort, firms' responses to applications and the characteristics of the jobs that are targeted.

Estimating Duration Dependence in Job Search: the Within-Estimation Duration Bias

single-authored paper

Demonstrating the existence of a (duration) bias when (net) duration dependence effects are estimated through fixed effects models, a commonly used approach when dynamic selection affects the sample of study.

Policy work (selected)
